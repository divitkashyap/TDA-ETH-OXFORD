In the United States, such schemes generally violate the federal securities laws and can result in significant civil and criminal penalties. Below are the primary laws and rules that cover this type of misconduct:

1. Securities Exchange Act of 1934

Section 10(b)
‚Ä¢ What it is: Section 10(b) of the Securities Exchange Act of 1934 is a broad anti-fraud provision.
‚Ä¢ Key point: It makes it unlawful to use or employ ‚Äúany manipulative or deceptive device or contrivance‚Äù in connection with the purchase or sale of any security.

SEC Rule 10b-5
‚Ä¢ What it is: Adopted under Section 10(b), Rule 10b-5 is one of the most important tools the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) uses to combat fraud.
‚Ä¢ Key point: It specifically prohibits (1) making any untrue statement of a material fact, (2) omitting to state a material fact necessary to make statements not misleading, and (3) engaging in any act, practice, or course of business that operates as a fraud or deceit upon any person in connection with the purchase or sale of a security.

How It Applies to ‚ÄúPump and Dump‚Äù:
‚Ä¢ If someone promotes a security with false or misleading information‚Äîor omits crucial negative information‚Äîto inflate the price artificially and then sells their stake at the inflated price, they can be liable under Rule 10b-5.

2. Securities Act of 1933

Section 17(a)
‚Ä¢ What it is: Section 17(a) is another broad anti-fraud provision that applies to the offer or sale of securities.
‚Ä¢ Key point: It prohibits fraud, deceit, and misrepresentation in the offer and sale of securities.

‚Ä¢ What it is: Sometimes called the ‚Äúanti-touting‚Äù rule.
‚Ä¢ Key point: Requires promoters and ‚Äúinfluencers‚Äù to disclose the nature and amount of compensation they are receiving for promoting a security. How It Applies to ‚ÄúPump and Dump‚Äù:
‚Ä¢ If an individual is paid to promote or tout a security without disclosing that fact, they can be violating Section 17(b).

‚Ä¢ What it is: Prohibits transactions in a security that create actual or apparent active trading or affect the price of a security for the purpose of inducing others to buy or sell.
‚Ä¢ Key point: Specifically targets market manipulation, such as wash trades, matched orders, or any scheme designed to artificially move a stock‚Äôs price.

‚Ä¢ Pump-and-dump schemes can involve orchestrated trades, mass promotional emails, social media blasts, etc., intended to create the false impression of strong market interest in a security.
